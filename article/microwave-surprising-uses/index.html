Your Microwave Is the Most Underrated Kitchen Tool — 10 Uses Beyond Reheating

Published: 2026-05-02~7 min read

The microwave sits on your counter, used three times a day to reheat coffee and leftover pasta, never asked to do more. This is like using a laptop solely as a calculator. Inside that box is technology that can cook, steam, dehydrate, infuse, proof, and extract — often faster and with less cleanup than the stovetop or oven. Here are ten specific, tested uses that will change how you see the most underrated appliance in your kitchen.

1. Peel an Entire Head of Garlic in 15 Seconds

This sounds fake. It is not. Place a whole head of garlic in the microwave. Microwave on high for 15 seconds (20 seconds if your microwave is under 900 watts). Remove carefully — it will be warm. Let it cool for 10 seconds, then hold the head by the root end and squeeze. The cloves will slide out of their skins with zero knife work, zero smashing, zero sticky garlic fingers. What happened: the microwaves heated the tiny amount of moisture inside each clove, creating steam between the clove and the papery skin. That steam separates the two instantly. You can peel an entire head in less time than it takes to peel two cloves the traditional way. Power: High, 15 seconds per head.

2. Revive Stale Bread in Under 30 Seconds

Stale bread is not dry bread — it is bread where the starch molecules have crystallized, a process called retrogradation. The moisture is still there, just locked into crystalline structures. Microwaving reheats the starch and breaks those crystals, releasing the trapped moisture and restoring softness. Wrap a slice of stale bread in a damp paper towel — the towel should be just damp, not dripping. Microwave on high for 10 seconds. Check it; add 5 seconds if needed. The bread emerges soft, steamy, and indistinguishable from fresh. This works on baguettes, dinner rolls, sandwich bread, and even croissants (though croissants need only 6-8 seconds). Critical: eat immediately. The starch will recrystallize within minutes, and the bread will actually become harder than before. This is a real-time trick, not a storage hack.

3. Steam Vegetables Faster Than Any Steamer Basket

No steamer basket, no pot, no waiting for water to boil. Place washed, chopped vegetables in a microwave-safe bowl. Add 1-2 tablespoons of water — just enough to create steam. Cover tightly with microwave-safe plastic wrap or a fitted lid (leave one small corner vented). Microwave on high. Timing guide: broccoli florets — 3 minutes; green beans — 3-4 minutes; carrots sliced into rounds — 3 minutes; asparagus spears — 2-3 minutes; spinach — 1 minute. The result is bright, tender-crisp vegetables that retain more nutrients than boiling because there is no water for water-soluble vitamins to leach into. Season after cooking with salt, pepper, and a pat of butter. Bonus: the bowl you cook in is the bowl you serve in — one dish to wash.

4. Make Crispy Potato Chips With No Oil

Yes, crispy chips. From a microwave. Without frying. Slice a potato as thinly and uniformly as possible — a mandoline is ideal, but a steady hand with a sharp knife works. Aim for 1-2mm thickness. Soak the slices in cold water for 5 minutes to remove surface starch (this is the crispness secret). Pat completely dry with paper towels — moisture is the enemy here. Arrange slices in a single layer on a piece of parchment paper placed on a microwave-safe plate. No overlapping. Lightly season with salt — do not add oil. Microwave on high for 3 minutes. Check for browning; add time in 30-second increments. Depending on your microwave wattage and slice thickness, total time is 4-6 minutes. At around the 4-minute mark, the slices will begin to brown and crisp dramatically. Remove when golden — they will continue to crisp as they cool. The result is a genuinely crunchy, oil-free potato chip. Flavored variations: sprinkle paprika, garlic powder, or rosemary before microwaving. Power: High. Watch carefully: the difference between perfect and burnt is about 15 seconds.

PRO TIP

A turntable microwave works better than a flatbed for chips because even rotation prevents hot spots. If your chips brown unevenly, your microwave likely has hot spots — rotate the plate 180 degrees halfway through cooking. You can also use a microwave crisper pan (the kind with a metalized surface that gets hot) for even better results, but plain parchment paper on a plate works 90% as well.

5. Toast Nuts and Seeds in 2 Minutes (No Pan, No Burning)

Toasting nuts on the stovetop requires constant attention — look away for 20 seconds and you have burnt almonds. The microwave does it more evenly and more forgivingly. Spread a single layer of nuts or seeds on a microwave-safe plate. Sunflower seeds: 1 minute on high, stir, 30 seconds more. Sliced almonds: 1.5 minutes, stir, 45 seconds more. Whole almonds: 2 minutes, stir, 1 minute more. Walnut halves: 1.5 minutes total, checking every 30 seconds. Pine nuts: 1 minute total, stir halfway. The nuts will not brown as deeply as in a pan, but they will smell fragrant and taste toasty. They also crisp up as they cool. This is the single fastest way to elevate any salad, oatmeal, or baked good with almost no effort. Key: nuts continue cooking after the microwave stops, so pull them when they are fragrant but slightly lighter than your target color.

6. Get Every Last Drop Out of Citrus Fruits

A cold lemon or lime yields about half the juice of a warm one. Microwave a whole lemon or lime on high for 15-20 seconds — until it is warm to the touch, not hot. Roll it firmly on the counter under your palm for 5 seconds. Cut and squeeze. You will get approximately 30-40% more juice than from a cold fruit. Why: heat breaks down the cell walls inside the fruit and thins the juice, reducing its viscosity so it flows out more easily. This works for lemons, limes, oranges, and grapefruits. It also works for garlic and ginger — 10 seconds of microwaving makes ginger peelable with a spoon rather than requiring a knife and patience.

7. Proof Bread Dough When Your Kitchen Is Cold

Yeast dough needs warmth to rise, and a drafty kitchen in winter kills your baking ambitions. Use the microwave as a proofing chamber. First, microwave a cup of water on high for 2 minutes until it boils and the microwave interior is warm and steamy. Remove the water. Place your covered bowl of dough inside the microwave. Close the door. The trapped warmth and humidity create the ideal proofing environment — roughly 80-85°F (27-29°C) with high humidity. Dough that would take 2 hours on a cold countertop will rise in 45 minutes. Do not turn the microwave on with the dough inside — the heat is provided by the pre-warmed environment, not by cooking. This technique transforms winter baking.

8. Fry Shallots and Garlic into Crispy Garnish Without a Wok of Oil

Thinly slice shallots or garlic as uniformly as possible — 1mm thickness for shallots, paper-thin for garlic. Toss with 1 tablespoon of neutral oil (canola, vegetable) per half cup of sliced shallots. Spread in a single layer on a microwave-safe plate lined with paper towel. Microwave on high for 2 minutes. Stir and redistribute. Continue microwaving in 1-minute intervals, stirring each time, until golden brown — about 5-7 minutes total for shallots, 3-4 minutes for garlic. Remove when slightly lighter than your target color; they darken as they cool. Drain on fresh paper towels and sprinkle with salt. These keep for a week in an airtight container and add instant crunch and flavor to soups, salads, stir-fries, and congee. The microwave essentially renders the water out and fries the slices in their own moisture-replaced oil. It works startlingly well.

9. Dry Fresh Herbs for Long-Term Storage

Fresh herbs go bad in days. Dried herbs from the grocery store are expensive and often flavorless. Microwaving dries fresh herbs in minutes while preserving far more flavor and color than air-drying or oven-drying. Wash herbs and pat absolutely dry — any remaining moisture will steam rather than dehydrate. Arrange leaves in a single layer between two paper towels on a microwave-safe plate. Microwave on high for 30 seconds. Check for dryness; if leaves are not brittle, continue in 15-second increments. Basil takes about 45-60 seconds total. Rosemary: 1-1.5 minutes total. Parsley: 45-60 seconds. Cilantro: 30-45 seconds. Thyme: 30-45 seconds. Leaves should crumble easily when fully dry. Store in an airtight jar away from light. Flavor is dramatically better than store-bought dried herbs. Power: High. Critical: leaves must be fully dry before storing, or mold will develop.

10. Disinfect and Deodorize Kitchen Sponges

Kitchen sponges are the most bacteria-dense objects in most homes — more than toilet seats. The USDA has confirmed that microwaving a wet sponge kills 99.9% of bacteria, including E. coli and salmonella. Method: thoroughly wet the sponge — it must be soaking wet, because a dry sponge can catch fire. Place it in the microwave. Microwave on high for 1 minute for a standard kitchen sponge, 90 seconds for larger sponges. Let it cool for 2 minutes before handling — it will be extremely hot. Do this every evening. Replace the sponge entirely every 2 weeks. This habit alone can significantly reduce kitchen-related illness. Warning: do not microwave sponges that contain metal scrub pads, and do not microwave a dry or barely-damp sponge — fire risk. Synthetic sponges can melt if overheated; 1 minute on high for a fully soaked sponge is safe. Natural cellulose sponges handle microwaving best.

WARNING

Never microwave anything metallic — no foil, no metal-rimmed plates, no twist ties, no takeout containers with metal handles. Never microwave sealed containers — pressure will build and they may explode. Never microwave eggs in their shells — they will explode (and cleaning exploded egg out of a microwave is a special kind of misery). Never microwave nothing — running an empty microwave can damage the magnetron. Always use microwave-safe containers: glass, ceramic, microwave-safe plastic labeled as such. Do not microwave single-use plastic containers (yogurt tubs, takeout containers) — they can warp and leach chemicals.

Why Microwaves Are Misunderstood

Microwaves do not cook from the inside out. That is a persistent myth. They penetrate food to a depth of about 1-1.5 inches (2.5-4 cm), exciting water molecules to generate heat. The inside of thick foods cooks via conduction from the heated outer layers — exactly like a conventional oven, just faster. And contrary to another myth, microwaving does not destroy nutrients more than other cooking methods. In fact, because microwave cooking is faster and uses less water, it often preserves more water-soluble vitamins than boiling or steaming on the stovetop. The microwave is not a second-rate shortcut — it is a fundamentally different and often superior cooking technology that happens to be exceptionally convenient.

Power Settings Actually Matter

Most people treat their microwave as an on-off switch: 100% power or nothing. This is like driving a car with only the accelerator fully floored or the engine off. Lower power settings do not reduce the microwave's output — they cycle the magnetron on and off. At 50% power, the microwave runs at full intensity but half the time, giving heat time to distribute evenly through the food during the off cycles. Use high power for: heating liquids, steaming vegetables, par-cooking dense items. Use medium (50-70%) for: melting butter or chocolate, softening cream cheese, cooking eggs, reheating delicate foods that easily overcook. Use low (30%) for: defrosting, proofing dough, gently warming leftovers that dry out easily. Learning your microwave's power levels is the single biggest upgrade you can make to your microwave game — and it requires zero new equipment.

The Bottom Line

The microwave is not just a reheating box. It is a steam chamber, a dehydrator, a proofing cabinet, a nut toaster, a sterilization device, and a rapid-infusion tool. These ten techniques take advantage of physics that was always there — you just needed someone to show you which buttons to push and for how long. Next time you reach for a pot, a pan, or your oven for a small task, ask yourself: could the microwave do this in a third of the time with half the cleanup? The answer, more often than you think, is yes.
