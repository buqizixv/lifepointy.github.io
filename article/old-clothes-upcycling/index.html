Don't Throw Away Old Clothes — 15 Things You Can Make in 10 Minutes

Published: 2026-05-05~7 min read

The average person throws away about 37 kilograms (81 pounds) of clothing per year. Most of that clothing is not actually worn out — it is just stained, torn in one spot, out of style, or slightly the wrong size. Before you fill another trash bag for the landfill, consider this: with a pair of scissors and ten minutes, most garments can become something genuinely useful. No sewing machine. No crafting experience. No special tools beyond what is already in your house. Here are fifteen projects, ranked from absolute-beginner to slightly-more-involved, all completable in roughly ten minutes.

Zero-Skill Projects (Literally Just Cutting)

1. T-Shirt Tote Bag — No Sewing Required

Difficulty: 1/10. Time: 5 minutes. Take an old T-shirt — heavier cotton works better than thin jersey. Lay it flat. Cut off both sleeves along the armhole seams, creating a tank-top shape. Now cut the neckline deeper — about 10-15 cm (4-6 inches) from the original neckline, following its curve to create a wider opening. This is the mouth of your bag. Turn the shirt inside out. Lay it flat again. Along the bottom hem, cut a series of vertical slits: about 8-10 cm (3-4 inches) long, spaced about 2 cm (0.75 inches) apart, through both layers of fabric. These are your fringe strips. Starting from one side, tie each front strip to its corresponding back strip in a double knot. Work your way across. Turn the bag right-side out. You now have a stretchy, washable, surprisingly strong tote bag. It holds 5-6 kg (11-13 lbs) of groceries easily. The knotted fringe becomes a decorative bottom detail. Pro tip: use a shirt with a graphic print and the design becomes the bag's focal point.

2. T-Shirt Yarn — The Universal Craft Material

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 8 minutes. T-shirt yarn (sometimes called "tarn") is a continuous fabric strip that can be knitted, crocheted, braided, or woven into rugs, baskets, plant hangers, and more. Lay a T-shirt flat. Cut off the hem at the bottom. Cut horizontally from one side seam to about 3 cm (1.2 inches) before the other side seam — stop before cutting all the way through. Continue cutting horizontal strips about 2 cm (0.75 inches) wide up the entire body of the shirt, stopping 3 cm before the opposite edge each time. You are creating a series of connected strips, like a zigzag. When the shirt is fully cut, open it up. You will see where the uncut edges connect the strips. Cut diagonally from the end of one strip to the start of the next — this connects all strips into one continuous strand. Pull the strand to stretch it; the fabric will curl in on itself, creating a round yarn-like cord. One adult large T-shirt yields about 15-20 meters (50-65 feet) of yarn. This yarn can be used for dozens of other projects: macrame plant hangers, braided dog toys, woven placemats, or chunky knit rugs. It is free, machine-washable, and comes in any color your old T-shirts happen to be.

3. No-Sew Crop Top or Tank Top Refresh

Difficulty: 1/10. Time: 3 minutes. A stained or overstretched T-shirt becomes a perfectly good crop top or workout tank with three cuts. Cut off the sleeves. Cut the neckline deeper or wider — whatever shape you prefer. Cut the bottom hem off to your desired crop length. Because T-shirt jersey does not fray, no hemming is required. The cut edges will curl slightly, which actually looks intentional — a design feature, not a flaw. For a racerback tank, after removing sleeves, cut a deep V into the back of the shirt instead of a symmetrical neckline. This costs zero dollars and rescues a shirt that would otherwise be unwearable. Bonus: the bottom portion you cut off can be used as a headband or wristband.

4. Sweater Pet Bed — Zero Sewing

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 10 minutes. You need an old chunky-knit sweater (wool or thick cotton works best) and a pillow or stuffing from an old cushion. Lay the sweater flat. Cut off the sleeves — set them aside for another project. Sewing is optional but not needed: if the sweater is long enough, fold the bottom opening inward and stuff the body of the sweater with pillow filling, old fabric scraps, or even crumpled newspaper in a pinch. For the neck opening, either tie it shut with a piece of one sleeve (cut into a strip) or simply fold it under and tuck it in. The result is a plush, machine-washable pet bed. For cats, leave the neck slightly open — they will burrow inside and treat it as a cave-bed. For dogs, close both ends and sew or tie them for a more structured cushion. The sweater's natural stretch conforms to the pet's body, and the fabric carries your scent, which is comforting to animals.

PRO TIP

The best sweaters for pet beds are 100% wool (felted — wash in hot water and dry on high heat first to shrink and tighten the knit) or thick cable-knit cotton. Thin acrylic sweaters work but are less durable. Avoid anything with loose sequins, beads, or buttons that a pet could chew off and swallow.

5. No-Sew Infinity Scarf from an Old T-Shirt

Difficulty: 1/10. Time: 3 minutes. Cut a T-shirt horizontally just below the armpits. Discard the top portion (or save for T-shirt yarn). You are left with a tube of fabric. Cut the bottom hem off. The remaining tube, when worn looped around your neck twice, becomes a soft, casual infinity scarf. The raw edges curl naturally and do not fray. Different T-shirt widths create different scarf volumes — an XL shirt makes a chunky winter scarf; a small shirt makes a lightweight summer neck wrap. This is the single fastest clothing transformation in existence — literally one cut, zero skill, and you have a new accessory.

Minimal-Skill Projects (Cutting + Tying or Gluing)

6. Jean Coasters and Placemats

Difficulty: 3/10. Time: 10 minutes for a set of 6 coasters. Old jeans have two things going for them as coasters: they are absorbent (water rings begone) and the denim texture is naturally non-slip. To make coasters: cut squares approximately 10 x 10 cm (4 x 4 inches) from the leg section of old jeans. Cut multiple squares — denim is thin enough that you want 2-3 layers per coaster for adequate absorbency. Layer the squares and use fabric glue to bond them together, or simply use them stacked loose — they grip each other well enough. For the edges, you can leave them raw for a frayed look (denim frays attractively over time and the fraying stops naturally), or apply a thin bead of fabric glue along the edges to seal them. To make placemats, use the entire leg section: cut a 30 x 45 cm (12 x 18 inch) rectangle, layer two pieces, and glue together. Coasters from one leg of jeans: approximately 12-15 coasters. Total cost: zero, unless you buy fabric glue (about $3).

7. Sock Phone Armband for Exercise

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 5 minutes. You need one old crew-length sock (tube socks work best). Cut off the toe section — you now have a fabric tube. Slide the tube onto your upper arm — it should fit snugly but not tight. Fold the tube in half lengthwise to create a double-layered pocket. Slide your phone into the fold. The sock fabric grips the phone securely, and the stretch keeps it pressed against your arm during running or workouts. For extra security, cut two small horizontal slits in the sock tube and thread a hair tie or elastic band through them to create a tighter band. This beats paying $15-25 for a commercial armband and uses a sock whose partner was lost to the laundry dimension. Specs: works best with crew socks or knee-high socks. Ankle socks are too short. The sock should be snug on your arm — if it is loose, use a smaller sock or fold the tube one extra time.

8. T-Shirt Produce Bags — Replace Plastic at the Grocery Store

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 5 minutes each. Using the same technique as project 1 (T-shirt tote bag), simply make a smaller version. Use a child's T-shirt or cut an adult shirt down to size. The resulting mesh-like bag (T-shirt jersey is naturally breathable) is perfect for carrying produce at the grocery store: onions, potatoes, apples, lemons. The knit material allows produce to breathe, preventing the premature spoilage that happens in sealed plastic produce bags. Make 5-6 in different sizes and keep them with your reusable shopping bags. They weigh almost nothing, scrunch down to pocket-sized, and are machine-washable when they get dirty. This single hack eliminates one of the most common single-use plastics in your household.

9. Braided Dog Toy from Old T-Shirts

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 8 minutes. Cut three strips of T-shirt fabric, each about 5 cm (2 inches) wide and 40-50 cm (16-20 inches) long — you can use the T-shirt yarn technique to get long continuous strips, or simply cut from the body of the shirt. Gather the three strips together and tie a tight knot at one end. Braid the three strips together firmly — a tight braid is more durable. Tie another tight knot at the other end. Trim any uneven ends. You now have a tug-of-war toy that dogs genuinely love. The fabric texture is satisfying for them to grip, it is soft on their gums, and unlike rope toys, it does not shed fibers that can tangle in their intestines. When it gets gross, throw it in the washing machine or simply make a new one — you have an endless supply of material. Safety note: supervise your dog with any toy. If the toy starts coming apart, discard it and make a new one. Do not let your dog eat fabric pieces.

10. Sock Coffee Cup Sleeve — No More Burnt Fingers

Difficulty: 1/10. Time: 2 minutes. Cut the toe section off an old sock. Cut the sock to about 8 cm (3 inches) in length — a cylinder of fabric. Slide it over your takeaway coffee cup or a glass jar used for hot drinks. The double layer of sock fabric insulates perfectly, and the stretch means it fits most cup sizes. It is washable (unlike cardboard sleeves), always available (unlike that one cardboard sleeve you lost), and uses a sock whose mate disappeared months ago. For a more polished look, use a colorful or patterned sock — people will ask where you bought it. The answer: your sock drawer, for free.

WARNING

When cutting clothing, especially thick fabrics like denim or wool, use sharp scissors. Dull scissors require more force, increasing the chance of slipping and cutting yourself. Fabric scissors (or any scissors you do not use for paper, which dulls blades quickly) work best. Keep your non-cutting hand well clear of the cutting line. If you are a complete beginner, make your first cuts slowly and deliberately — speed comes with practice, but safety does not.

Slightly-More-Involved Projects (Still Under 15 Minutes)

11. Denim Pocket Wall Organizer

Difficulty: 4/10. Time: 12 minutes. The back pockets of jeans are perfectly sized mini-pouches — and they are already sewn. Cut out the entire back pocket from a pair of old jeans, leaving about 2 cm (0.75 inches) of extra fabric around the edges. Do this for 3-4 pairs of jeans. Arrange the pockets on a piece of cardboard, a thin wooden board, or even a sturdy piece of denim from a jean leg — this is your backing. Glue the pockets onto the backing using strong fabric glue or a hot glue gun. Let dry. Hang on the wall with adhesive strips or nails. Use for: remote controls, pens and scissors in a home office, makeup brushes in a bathroom, mail and keys by the front door, or small toy storage in a kid's room. Each pocket costs nothing and would cost $8-15 if purchased as a wall organizer. This is genuinely one of the most useful things you can make from old jeans in under 15 minutes.

12. Sweater Leg Warmers or Arm Warmers

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 8 minutes. Cut the sleeves off an old sweater. Trim them to your desired length — for leg warmers, keep the full sleeve; for arm warmers, cut to about 25-30 cm (10-12 inches). The cuff of the sweater sleeve naturally becomes the bottom cuff of your warmer. For the other end, you can leave it raw (knit does not fray badly) or fold it over and secure with fabric glue. Sweater sleeves are already the right shape — they are essentially pre-made tubes in exactly the right proportions. Cable-knit and ribbed sweaters make the most attractive warmers. These are genuinely useful in cold weather, cost $15-30 if purchased, and take longer to describe than to actually make.

13. T-Shirt Produce Hanging Basket

Difficulty: 3/10. Time: 12 minutes. This is T-shirt yarn put to use. Make a long continuous strand of T-shirt yarn (project 2). You will need about 10 meters (33 feet) — roughly half a large T-shirt's worth. The technique: create a series of chain knots (like a very simple macrame chain) until you have a rope about 1.5 meters (5 feet) long. Coil this rope into a flat spiral, and use small dabs of hot glue between coils to hold the spiral in a bowl shape. Alternatively, weave the T-shirt yarn through a wire basket frame to create a soft liner. Hang the resulting basket in the kitchen to hold onions, garlic, or fruit. It costs zero dollars, is fully washable, and looks intentionally rustic rather than just cheap — people pay $25-40 for similar "boho" hanging baskets.

14. Shirt Collar Bookmarks

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 5 minutes for a set of 4. Cut the collar off an old button-down shirt, including about 5 cm (2 inches) of fabric below the collar. Trim the cut edge straight. Apply a thin layer of fabric glue to the raw edge and fold it over to create a clean finish — or leave it raw and let it fray slightly for a shabby-chic look. The collar's natural stiffness (from the interfacing inside) makes it a perfect bookmark. The button at the front becomes a decorative detail. Slide it over the corner of a page. These make excellent gifts, cost nothing to produce, and each shirt yields one bookmark plus four more if you also use the cuffs. Old dress shirts with worn cuffs or frayed collars — the exact reason you were going to throw them out — are ironically your best candidates for this project.

15. Sweater Pillow Cover — The Easiest Throw Pillow Ever

Difficulty: 2/10. Time: 10 minutes. Take an old sweater. Cut off the sleeves. Cut a rectangle from the body of the sweater that is about 5 cm (2 inches) larger on all sides than your pillow insert — or simply use the entire sweater body if it is roughly pillow-sized. Place the pillow insert in the center of the fabric rectangle. Pull the corners of the fabric around to the back of the pillow and tie them in pairs — top-left to bottom-left, top-right to bottom-right, or simply gather all four corners and tie. For a more finished look, use fabric glue to seal the open edges on the back, or stitch them with a simple running stitch. The result is a cozy, textured throw pillow that looks like a $40 catalog item. Cable-knit cream sweaters make the most Instagram-worthy pillows. This is one of the few projects on this list where people will genuinely not believe you made it yourself — until you show them the armholes.

What Fabrics Work Best for Which Projects

Not all clothing is created equal for upcycling. Cotton jersey (T-shirts): The holy grail. Does not fray, stretches, cuts clean, available in every color. Best for: bags, yarn, scarves, produce bags, dog toys. Denim: Frays decoratively, very durable, thick. Best for: coasters, placemats, organizers, heavy-duty patches. Wool and chunky knit sweaters: Warm, feltable, thick. Best for: pet beds, pillow covers, leg warmers. Button-down shirt fabric (woven cotton): Frays badly if not hemmed, but has excellent structure. Best for: bookmarks, small organizers (where edges can be glued), patches. Stretchy athletic wear: Hard to cut cleanly, edges curl aggressively. Best for: headbands, wristbands, rag cloths. Flannel and fleece: Does not fray, soft, warm. Best for: pet beds, scarves, cleaning cloths.

Before You Cut: The Ten-Second Sort

When going through clothes to discard, sort into three piles. Pile 1: genuinely worn out — holes everywhere, fabric disintegrating, stained beyond redemption. These become cleaning rags, then trash. Pile 2: donate-able — good condition, just unwanted. These go to charity. Pile 3: the sweet spot — one stain, one tear, one area of wear, but otherwise solid fabric. Pile 3 is your upcycling material. One stain does not ruin a whole T-shirt — cut around it. One torn knee does not ruin jeans — the upper legs have meters of clean denim. One snagged sweater is still 80% perfect wool fabric. This sorting method alone prevents about half of unnecessary clothing disposal.

The Bottom Line

Fifteen projects. Average time: under 10 minutes each. Required tools: scissors, maybe fabric glue ($3). Required skills: none. These projects transform clothing that was headed for a landfill into: tote bags, produce bags, yarn, scarves, coasters, placemats, pet beds, pillow covers, armbands, cup sleeves, wall organizers, leg warmers, bookmarks, dog toys, and hanging baskets. At retail prices, that is roughly $150-250 worth of household goods manufactured from material you were about to throw away. More importantly, each project eliminates the need to buy a new product — the most sustainable item is the one you never purchase in the first place. Old clothes are not garbage. They are pre-cut fabric with the hard part — the sewing and shaping — already done. You just need to see it.
