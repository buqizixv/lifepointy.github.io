The Unwritten Rules of Getting Promoted: 7 Things Your Manager Won't Tell You

Published: 2026-05-20~7 min read

I once asked a senior executive who had promoted over 200 people in her career what percentage of promotions were based on job performance versus other factors. She thought for a moment and said: "I would say about 30% is the actual work you deliver. The other 70% is whether the people in the room during the promotion calibration meeting can remember your name and associate it with something positive." She was not being cynical. She was describing the reality of how organizations make people decisions. If you want to get promoted, you need to understand that reality and operate within it — not complain about how unfair it is.

PRO TIP: Think of your career as having two job descriptions. Job 1 is the one in your offer letter — the tasks you are paid to perform. Job 2 is "getting promoted" — a completely different set of activities that nobody puts in writing but that determines whether you advance. Most people spend 100% of their energy on Job 1 and 0% on Job 2, then wonder why they get passed over.

Unwritten Rule 1: Visibility Math — You Cannot Get Promoted If Decision Makers Do Not Know Your Name

Here is a brutal exercise: write down the names of the people who will be in the room when your promotion is decided. This is typically your manager, your skip-level manager (your boss's boss), and 2-4 other managers at the same level as your skip-level — the "calibration committee" that meets to ensure promotions are fair across teams. Now, next to each name, estimate how many times that person has heard your name associated with something positive in the past 12 months. If the answer is "zero" for any of them, you have a visibility deficit that no amount of excellent work in the trenches can overcome.

The fix: create what I call a "visibility rhythm." Once a month, ensure one of your accomplishments reaches one of the decision makers on your list. This can happen through: (a) your manager mentioning your work in a skip-level meeting (ask them to — "Would you be comfortable flagging the X project results in your next meeting with [VP]?"), (b) you presenting something at a team all-hands or town hall, (c) you sending a brief, non-boastful update to a distribution list that includes decision makers ("Quick update on the X initiative — here is where we landed and what we learned"), or (d) you contributing a data point or insight in a meeting where a decision maker is present.

The monthly cadence is critical. One visibility event feels like a fluke. Two feels like a coincidence. Three or more starts to form a pattern — and patterns are what decision makers use to form impressions. By the time the calibration meeting happens, you want at least 6-8 positive associations lodged in each decision maker's mind.

Unwritten Rule 2: Build Your Decision-Maker Map

Not all decision makers are equal. In most organizations, the promotion decision follows a "one veto, one champion" model. You need at least one person in the room who will actively argue for your promotion (your champion), and you need zero people who will actively argue against it (a veto). Everyone else can be neutral.

Your map should classify each committee member as: Champion (will advocate for you unprompted), Supporter (will agree if asked but will not initiate), Neutral (has no opinion one way or the other), Skeptic (has heard something negative or has no awareness of you at all — functionally the same thing), or Blocker (will actively argue against your promotion). Your goal: turn your manager into a Champion (if they are not already, address this immediately — see Rule 6), convert at least one Supporter into a second Champion, move Skeptics to Neutral by feeding them visibility, and identify any Blockers early so you can either neutralize their concerns or, if that is not possible, have your Champion prepared to counter their arguments in the calibration meeting.

WARNING: Do not attempt to lobby committee members directly. "Hi, I want to get promoted, can you support me?" is career suicide. Visibility must feel organic. The decision makers must feel like they arrived at their positive impression of you independently. If they sense you are running a campaign, the campaign backfires. The art is making strategic visibility look like natural competence.

Unwritten Rule 3: The Pre-Promotion Campaign Starts 6 Months Before Review

Promotions are not awarded in the review meeting. They are ratified in the review meeting. The actual decision was made weeks or months earlier, in a series of informal conversations between managers and their peers. By the time the calibration meeting happens, the outcome is largely pre-determined. The meeting is for rubber-stamping and ensuring consistency, not for genuine deliberation.

Your pre-promotion campaign has three tracks that run in parallel, starting 6 months before your review:

Track 1 — The Manager Track: You tell your manager explicitly that you want to be promoted in the next cycle. This conversation should happen 6 months out and sound like this: "I want to be promoted to [target title] in the [spring/fall] cycle. Can we work together over the next six months to make sure I check every box by then? Specifically, what are the gaps you see between where I am today and where I need to be?" Three things happen with this conversation: (1) your manager now knows your intent and can plan for it, (2) you get a gap analysis that tells you exactly what to work on, (3) your manager becomes psychologically committed to your promotion because they co-created the plan with you. People do not like to abandon plans they helped design.

Track 2 — The Evidence Track: You spend the next six months systematically closing each gap your manager identified. For each gap, produce concrete, documented evidence. If the gap was "cross-functional leadership," you lead a project involving two other teams and save the emails, the slide decks, and the thank-you messages from stakeholders. By the time the review comes, you have a portfolio, not a request.

Track 3 — The Narrative Track: You shape the story that will be told about you in the calibration meeting. Your manager will need to summarize your case in 2-3 minutes. Do the work for them. Six weeks before the review, send your manager a one-page document titled "[Your Name] Promotion Packet" containing: a 3-sentence summary of why you are ready, 5-7 bullet points of concrete evidence organized by the promotion criteria, and a list of 3-5 people who can provide supporting input (with their permission). Your manager can now walk into the calibration meeting with a pre-built case. This reduces their cognitive load and increases the chance they will advocate effectively.

Unwritten Rule 4: The 3 Questions You Must Ask 6 Months Before Review

During the Manager Track conversation (Rule 3, Track 1), you must get answers to three specific questions. Do not leave the conversation until you have concrete, specific answers to all three:

Question 1: "What are the exact, written criteria for promotion to [target title]?" Most companies have a formal promotion rubric — a document that lists the competencies and behaviors expected at each level. Get it. If your company does not have one, ask your manager to list the criteria they would use if they had to write a promotion case for someone at that level. You need a checklist. You cannot hit a target you cannot see.

Question 2: "Who else needs to support this promotion besides you?" This gives you the decision-maker map (Rule 2). Many managers will name people you did not expect — a peer manager whose team you interact with, a product lead, a client executive. Knowing the full list prevents surprises and lets you build visibility with the right people.

Question 3: "Is there anything — any concern, any hesitation, any area where you think the committee might push back — that we should address now rather than discovering it in the meeting?" This question is gold because it gives your manager permission to voice doubts they might otherwise keep to themselves until the calibration meeting, where they would surface as a reason to delay. Address those doubts now, with six months to fix them, rather than hearing about them for the first time when the promotion is denied.

PRO TIP: After the Manager Track conversation, send a follow-up email summarizing what you discussed: "Thanks for the conversation. Confirming my understanding: the promotion criteria are [list], the gaps to close are [list], and the key stakeholders are [names]. I will work on these and check in with you monthly on progress. Sound right?" This creates the paper trail that makes it harder for the criteria to shift later — which they sometimes do when managers get uncomfortable with the reality of promoting someone.

Unwritten Rule 5: Strategic Failure — The Counter-Intuitive Career Accelerator

This is the most counter-intuitive rule in the playbook, and possibly the most powerful. High performers who have never visibly struggled with anything are actually harder to promote than people who have faced a challenge and overcome it. Why? Because promotion committees worry about the "Peter Principle" — the idea that people get promoted until they reach their level of incompetence. If all they have ever seen from you is smooth success, they wonder: "Yes, but what happens when things go wrong at the next level?" They cannot assess your resilience, your problem-solving under pressure, or your ability to navigate organizational complexity — because you have never demonstrated those things.

Strategic failure means deliberately taking on a project that is slightly outside your comfort zone — hard enough that you might stumble, visible enough that the stumble gets noticed, and recoverable enough that you can demonstrate a turnaround. You then navigate the stumble publicly, document what you learned, and present the recovery as a case study in resilience. The narrative becomes: "[Name] took on [hard project], hit [specific obstacle], figured out [creative solution], and delivered [result despite the setback]." That narrative is far more compelling to a promotion committee than "[Name] did everything perfectly."

How to do it: Volunteer for a project that is important but troubled — an initiative that has been languishing, a client relationship that needs repair, a process that everyone complains about but nobody fixes. These projects have built-in obstacles. When you encounter the obstacle, do not hide it. Communicate it: "We hit X issue. Here is what I think is happening, here are three possible paths forward, my recommendation is path B, but I want input before I commit." This demonstrates exactly the kind of judgment and communication senior roles require.

Unwritten Rule 6: Make Your Manager Your Champion — or Find a New One

If your manager is not actively advocating for your promotion, you will not get promoted — period. The calibration committee takes cues from the direct manager. If the manager is lukewarm, the committee reads that as a veto. If the manager is enthusiastic, the committee is inclined to agree unless there is a glaring objection. The manager is the single point of failure in the promotion process.

How to tell if your manager is your champion: they bring up your career development without you prompting them, they proactively give you stretch assignments that build toward the next level, they introduce you to senior people, they share credit with you in public settings, and when you ask about promotion, they give you specific, actionable feedback — not vague statements like "just keep doing what you are doing." Vague feedback means they have not thought about your promotion seriously. Specific feedback means they have.

If your manager is not your champion, you have two options. Option A: make them your champion using the Manager Track conversation from Rule 3. Some managers are not champions simply because nobody has ever asked them to be. The structured conversation wakes them up to their role. Option B: if you have tried Option A and they remain disengaged, find a new manager — either by transferring teams internally or by leaving the company. Staying under a manager who will not advocate for you is career stagnation by choice.

Unwritten Rule 7: Real Promotion Timelines (and How to Compress Them)

Here is what most companies will not tell you openly: the typical promotion timeline from individual contributor to first-level manager is 3-5 years. From first-level manager to director is another 3-5 years. From director to VP is 5-8 years. These are not rules — they are averages. The fastest promotes compress these timelines by roughly 40-50%. How? They change jobs every 2-3 years within the same company, taking lateral moves into higher-visibility roles before pivoting upward. They join a high-growth team or division where new management roles are being created constantly. And they make their ambitions known early — not in a demanding way, but in a way that lets the system plan around them.

The single biggest accelerant is joining a growing organization. In a stable or shrinking org, promotions are zero-sum — someone has to leave for you to move up. In a growing org, promotions are positive-sum — new roles are being created, and the organization needs people to fill them. If your division is not growing, your promotion timeline is inherently longer, no matter how good you are. Factor this into your career calculations honestly.

PRO TIP: The second biggest accelerant is building a successor. One of the hidden barriers to promotion is what I call "the indispensability trap." If you are the only person who knows how to do your job, your manager cannot afford to promote you out of it — promoting you would create a hole they cannot fill. The fix: identify and train someone who can replace you. Document your processes. Build redundancy. When your manager realizes that promoting you does not create a crisis, their resistance drops significantly. Counter-intuitively, the easiest way to get promoted is to make yourself replaceable in your current role.

The 70/30 Reality, Revisited

Let us return to the opening statistic: 30% of your promotion is the work you deliver, 70% is whether decision makers know your name and associate it with something positive. This is not a call to ignore your actual work. Without solid delivery, the visibility will not hold up under scrutiny. But solid delivery without visibility will not get you promoted either. The people who advance are the ones who do both — excellent work AND strategic visibility. Not one or the other. Both. The seven rules in this guide are the operating manual for the visibility half of the equation. Execute them with the same discipline you bring to your actual job, and you will stop being surprised when other people get promoted instead of you.
